## Course Overview

**AI Fluency** is the ability to collaborate with AI systems in ways that are **effective, efficient, ethical, and safe**. This course, developed by Rick Dakan, Joseph Feller, and Anthropic, teaches a durable framework for human-AI collaboration — not just tips and tricks, but lasting competencies that remain relevant as AI technology evolves.

The course centers on the **4D Framework**: four core competencies — **Delegation, Description, Discernment, and Diligence** — that apply across all three modes of AI engagement: **Automation** (AI executes tasks), **Augmentation** (human-AI co-creation), and **Agency** (AI works independently with your guidance).

By the end of the course, you'll have a structured way to think about when to use AI, how to communicate with it, how to evaluate its outputs, and how to engage with it responsibly.

### Lesson 2: Why Do We Need AI Fluency?

**Key Concepts:**
- AI Fluency involves practical skills, knowledge, insights, and values — not just technical know-how
- Three modes of AI engagement:
  - **Automation:** AI completes specific tasks based on your instructions
  - **Augmentation:** You and AI collaborate as creative thinking and task execution partners
  - **Agency:** You configure AI to work independently, shaping its knowledge and behavior patterns rather than giving it specific tasks

**Practical Tips:**
- Recognize which mode you're in (Automation, Augmentation, or Agency) — it changes how you should engage
- Agency-level collaboration requires more upfront setup but enables more autonomous AI work

  - **Delegation:** Thoughtfully deciding what work to do yourself vs. with AI
  - **Description:** Communicating clearly and effectively with AI systems
  - **Discernment:** Evaluating AI outputs and behavior with a critical eye

**Practical Tips:**
- Apply all 4Ds to any significant AI-assisted project — they complement each other
- Self-assess: which of the 4Ds do you feel most confident in? Which needs development?
- Use the 4D Framework as a lens when reviewing past AI interactions that didn't go well

### Lesson 4: Generative AI Fundamentals

**Key Concepts:**
- **Generative AI** creates new content (text, images, code) rather than just analyzing existing data
- Large Language Models (LLMs) were made possible by three developments:
  1. **Algorithmic breakthroughs** — especially the transformer architecture
  2. **Vast training datasets** — large amounts of digital text and data
  3. **Dramatic increases in computational power**
- AI learns in two stages: **pre-training** (finding patterns across billions of examples) and **fine-tuning** (learning to follow instructions and be helpful)
- Key concepts: context windows, emergent capabilities

**Practical Tips:**
- Understanding how LLMs are trained helps explain their behaviors and quirks
- Context windows limit how much an AI can "remember" within a single conversation — keep this in mind for long tasks

### Lesson 5: Capabilities & Limitations

**Key Concepts:**
- **Capabilities:** Versatility across language tasks, maintaining conversational flow, switching between diverse tasks without retraining, connecting to external tools
- **Limitations:**
  - **Knowledge cutoff dates** — AI doesn't know about recent events
  - **Hallucinations** — AI can generate factually incorrect outputs confidently
  - **Context window constraints** — limited working memory per conversation
  - **Reasoning challenges** — struggles with complex multi-step logic
- The most effective applications combine human and AI strengths
- Humans provide: critical thinking, judgment, creativity, and ethical oversight

**Practical Tips:**
- Always verify AI outputs on factual matters — hallucinations are real and common
- For tasks beyond the knowledge cutoff, provide the AI with relevant up-to-date information yourself
- Think of AI as a collaborator with complementary strengths, not a replacement for human judgment
- Use AI for tasks where its versatility shines; handle complex ethical/reasoning tasks yourself

### Lesson 6: A Closer Look at Delegation

**Key Concepts:**
- **Delegation** = deciding what to do yourself, what to collaborate on with AI, and what to let AI handle independently
- Three components of Delegation:
  1. **Problem Awareness:** Understand your goals and the nature of the work before involving AI
  2. **Platform Awareness:** Know what different AI systems can and cannot do
  3. **Task Delegation:** Strategically divide work between you and AI based on strengths
- Effective delegation requires **both** domain expertise and understanding of AI capabilities
- The goal is the most effective human-AI partnership — not automating everything

**Practical Tips:**
- Before starting a task, ask: *Should I do this? Should we do this together? Can AI do this independently?*
- Build Platform Awareness by experimenting across different AI tools and noting their strengths
- Match tasks to where AI excels: drafting, summarizing, brainstorming — not blind factual authority
- Maintain ownership of the parts that require your unique expertise, judgment, or accountability

### Lesson 7: Project Planning and Delegation

**Key Concepts:**
- Delegation is best practiced on a real, **multi-step project** — not just isolated tasks
- A good practice project is: substantial (multiple task types), manageable (~1 hour of work), and genuinely interesting to you

**Practical Tips:**

- Break the project into major tasks, then make explicit delegation decisions for each: you, together, or AI independently
- Write down your delegation plan before starting — it forces clarity and reveals assumptions
- Revisit the plan as you work; good delegation evolves as you learn more

### Lesson 8: A Closer Look at Description

**Key Concepts:**
- **Description** = communicating with AI in ways that create a productive collaborative environment
- Goes beyond "writing prompts" — it's about building a shared understanding
- Three components:
  1. **Product Description:** Define what you want — outputs, format, audience, style
  2. **Process Description:** Guide *how* the AI should approach your request (the method matters as much as the goal)
  3. **Performance Description:** Define the AI's behavior — concise or detailed, challenging or supportive, formal or casual
- AI cannot read your mind — quality of results depends directly on clarity of communication
- Clear upfront description saves time and leads to better results

**Practical Tips:**
- Before sending a prompt, check: have you addressed Product, Process, *and* Performance?
- Don't just describe the output — describe how you want the AI to work with you
- If a result is off, look first at your description before blaming the AI
- Use the "bad prompt makeover" exercise: take a vague prompt and add product/process/performance detail

### Lesson 9: Effective Prompting Techniques

**Key Concepts:**
- **Prompt engineering** = designing effective instructions for AI systems, combining communication principles with AI-specific techniques
- Six foundational prompting techniques:
  1. **Give context** — be specific about what you want, why you want it, and relevant background
  2. **Show examples** — demonstrate the output style or format you're looking for
  3. **Specify constraints** — define format, length, tone, and other requirements explicitly
  4. **Break complex tasks into steps** — guide the AI through multi-step reasoning
  5. **Ask the AI to think first** — give it space to work through its process before answering
  6. **Define the AI's role or tone** — specify how you want it to communicate
- Successful prompting is **iterative**, not a one-shot exercise

**Practical Tips:**
- Use the "secret weapon": ask the AI to help improve your own prompt
- When a response misses the mark, identify *which* of the six techniques was missing or weak
- Provide clear task overviews, format specs, explicit constraints, and relevant background in every substantive prompt
- For complex tasks, explicitly walk the AI through steps rather than dumping the whole problem at once
- Save effective prompts as templates for repeated tasks

### Lesson 10: A Closer Look at Discernment

**Key Concepts:**
- **Discernment** = thoughtfully evaluating AI outputs, processes, and behaviors
- Discernment is the flip side of Description — after communicating clearly, you evaluate what you receive
- Three components:
  1. **Product Discernment:** Evaluate the quality of actual outputs — accuracy, appropriateness, coherence, relevance
  2. **Process Discernment:** Assess *how* the AI arrived at its output — look for logical errors, attention gaps, inappropriate reasoning
  3. **Performance Discernment:** Evaluate how the AI behaved during collaboration — was its style effective for your needs?
- Discernment and Description work together in a **continuous feedback loop**
- Even the most advanced AI benefits from human judgment and oversight

**Practical Tips:**
- Don't just accept output that *looks* right — evaluate it in your domain of expertise
- For important work, explicitly check: Is this accurate? Did the AI miss anything? Did it follow the right reasoning?
- Evaluate not just *what* the AI produced but *how* it got there (process discernment)
- If you're not an expert in the content area, seek external verification or use AI to cross-check itself
- Watch for confident-sounding outputs — hallucinations often feel authoritative

---

### Lesson 11: The Description-Discernment Loop

**Key Concepts:**
- Description and Discernment don't work in isolation — they form a **continuous feedback loop**
- The loop: Describe clearly → Evaluate critically → Refine description → Repeat
- This iterative process produces results that exceed what either humans or AI could achieve alone
- Integrate your own expertise and judgment at each step — don't just accept and pass along

**Practical Tips:**
- Treat each AI interaction as an iteration, not a one-shot transaction
- After discerning a problem, go back and improve your description — then try again
- For each task in a project, explicitly cycle through: Describe → Receive → Discern → Refine
- The highest-value work happens in the refinement loop, not the first prompt

### Lesson 12: A Closer Look at Diligence

**Key Concepts:**
- **Diligence** = taking responsibility for our AI collaborations — the ethical and safety dimension
- While the other 3Ds address effectiveness and efficiency, Diligence addresses ethics and safety
- Three components:
  1. **Creation Diligence:** Be thoughtful about which AI systems you choose and how you engage with them
  2. **Transparency Diligence:** Be open and honest about AI's role in your work with everyone who needs to know
  3. **Deployment Diligence:** Take ownership of verifying and vouching for AI-assisted outputs you share with others
- Different contexts (personal, academic, professional) have different disclosure expectations
- A **Diligence Statement** is a transparent acknowledgment of AI's role in your work, plus your commitment to responsibility for the final output

**Practical Tips:**
- Before using an AI system, ask: Is this the right tool? What are its known biases or limitations?
- Be proactive about disclosure — don't wait to be asked if AI helped with your work
- You own the output. Even if AI wrote the first draft, your name on it means you're responsible for its accuracy and appropriateness
- Draft a Diligence Statement for any significant AI-assisted work: what AI did, how you verified it, and what you take responsibility for
- Develop personal guidelines for AI use that you can apply consistently across contexts

## The 4D Framework

The **4D Framework** is the core of AI Fluency. It consists of four competencies that together enable effective, efficient, ethical, and safe human-AI collaboration. They apply across all three modes of AI use: Automation, Augmentation, and Agency.

---

### 🎯 Delegation — *Deciding what to do with AI*

**The question it answers:** What should I do myself, what should we do together, and what can AI handle independently?

**Three components:**
- **Problem Awareness** — Understand your goals and the full scope of the work before bringing AI in
- **Platform Awareness** — Know the capabilities and limitations of the AI systems available to you
- **Task Delegation** — Strategically distribute work between you and AI to leverage each party's strengths

**Why it matters:** Effective delegation requires domain expertise + AI knowledge. The goal is not to automate everything, but to create the best human-AI partnership for each specific task.

---

### ✍️ Description — *Communicating clearly with AI*

**The question it answers:** How do I articulate what I need so AI can actually deliver it?

**Three components:**
- **Product Description** — Define the output: what it is, its format, intended audience, and style
- **Process Description** — Define how AI should approach the task: what method, what steps, what emphasis
- **Performance Description** — Define how AI should behave: tone, level of detail, collaborative style

**Why it matters:** AI cannot read your mind. The quality of results directly reflects the quality of your communication. Clear description up front saves iteration time and produces better outcomes.

---

### 🔍 Discernment — *Evaluating AI outputs critically*

**The question it answers:** Is what I received actually good, accurate, and appropriate?

**Three components:**
- **Product Discernment** — Evaluate the output for accuracy, coherence, relevance, and appropriateness
- **Process Discernment** — Evaluate *how* the AI approached the task: was its reasoning sound? Did it miss anything?
- **Performance Discernment** — Evaluate whether the AI's communication style and behavior served the collaboration

**Why it matters:** Even advanced AI systems hallucinate, miss nuance, or reason poorly. Human judgment and domain expertise remain essential for evaluating AI work. Discernment and Description form a continuous improvement loop.

---

### 🛡️ Diligence — *Engaging with AI responsibly*

**The question it answers:** Am I using AI ethically, honestly, and responsibly?

**Three components:**
- **Creation Diligence** — Choose AI systems thoughtfully; be aware of their biases and limitations
- **Transparency Diligence** — Be honest with relevant parties about AI's role in your work
- **Deployment Diligence** — Take ownership of AI-assisted outputs you share; verify before you vouch

**Why it matters:** Diligence is what makes AI collaboration trustworthy — for you, your colleagues, and the people affected by your work. Different contexts have different norms, but the responsibility to understand and meet those expectations is always yours.

## Top 15 Practical Tips (Consolidated)

1. **Treat AI as a collaborative partner, not a vending machine.** Approach it as an interactive system you communicate with, not a database you query.

2. **Know which mode you're in.** Are you using AI for Automation, Augmentation, or Agency? Each requires a different level of engagement and oversight.

3. **Before involving AI, get clear on your goal.** Problem Awareness — understanding what you're trying to achieve — is the foundation of effective Delegation.

4. **Always specify Product, Process, AND Performance in your description.** Most failed prompts are missing at least one of these three dimensions.

5. **Use the "secret weapon": ask AI to improve your prompt.** If results are poor, share your prompt with Claude and ask how to make it more effective.

6. **Break complex tasks into explicit steps.** Don't give AI one giant problem — guide it through the reasoning in stages.

7. **Ask the AI to "think first" before answering.** Giving AI space to work through its process often produces more accurate and thoughtful outputs.

8. **Verify factual claims — hallucinations are real.** AI can state incorrect information confidently. For anything consequential, fact-check independently.

9. **Evaluate outputs in your domain.** You know your field. Use that expertise when applying Discernment — don't just accept polished-looking output.

10. **Treat every AI interaction as an iteration.** The first response is a starting point, not the final product. Refine through the Description-Discernment loop.

11. **Save effective prompts as templates.** When you find a prompt structure that works well for a recurring task, document it for reuse.

12. **Be proactive about transparency.** Disclose AI's role in your work without waiting to be asked — in academic, professional, and public contexts.

13. **You own the output.** Regardless of how much AI contributed, your name means your responsibility. Verify before you vouch.

14. **Build a Personal AI Policy.** Write down your own principles for responsible AI use — it forces clarity and consistency across contexts.

15. **Develop your weakest 4D first.** Self-assess across Delegation, Description, Discernment, and Diligence. Focus deliberate practice on the competency that most limits your current AI collaboration.
